Top 10 Birds I've Seen

  1. Red-cockaded Woodpecker
  2. Spoonbill
  3. Western Tanager
  4. Painted Bunting
  5. Steller's Jay
  6. Loggerhead Shrike
  7. Scissor-tailed Flycatcher
  8. Bald Eagle
  9. Belted Kingfisher
  10. Broad-tailed Hummingbird
For all of my life, I have loved the outdoors. I grew up more or less in the country, running down gravel roads barefoot or exploring the woods behind our house. Somewhere along the line, I found a love for birds that has only grown through the years. Being a military spouse for 13 years gave me the opportunity to live in 3 different states, not including Missouri, and that allowed me to see unique birds in each state. The following is a list of my top 10 favorite birds I've seen in person in the wild, along with random stories and facts. It's probably worth noting that none of these pictures are ones I took (except for the ones that appear with the bold text), but they do a wonderful job of showing the beauty of each bird.

1. Red-cockaded Woodpecker

The Red-cockaded Woodpecker (RCW) isn't the most spectacular-looking bird, but it made #1 on my list because this species of woodpecker is endangered and some small, isolated groups are near extinction, so it's rare to see them. While living in Georgia, I went with two friends to a gopher tortoise preserve where they are also trying to help the RWC population. RCWs are the only woodpeckers to build their homes in live trees. Once they find their preferred tree, they begin making their home, which they return to every year, fully finishing their home after 7 years. RCWs prefer live trees to use for protection; they peck holes in the bark around the tree to cause sap to run out, which deters predators like snakes from climbing into their cavities and eating their eggs or fledglings. Older "siblings" will also return to help raise the next brood of their parents, which I think is pretty cool. For me, getting to see RCWs in the wild was a very rare and special event.

2. Roseate Spoonbill

The Roseate Spoonbill made #2 on my list because.... well, just look at it! This bird is so unique to me. They mostly live along the southern coasts of Florida, Texas, and Central and South America. I was pretty excited when I learned that a few decided to visit a wild bird sanctuary in South Carolina, not far from where I lived at the time in Georgia. I went there with my friend and her husband, who are both wildlife biologists, and we managed to find three juvenile male roaseate spoonbills. They were hanging out with hundreds of wood storks eating fish in the lowered ponds at the sanctuary. Since they weren't full-grown adults, their feathers weren't as bright pink as the one in the image, but their feathers were a soft, rosy shade of pink. I will never forget seeing these fun-looking birds and I'm so grateful I had the opportunity to see them in real life; otherwise, I'm not sure I would've ever known of their existence!

3. Western Tanager

For my third favorite bird, I chose the Western Tanager. I've only seen one in person once, and it was pretty magical. From the end of 2017 to early 2021, we lived in Fort Carson, CO, in the shadow of Cheyenne Mountain. (Ever heard of NORAD? I could see the entrance from my backyard!) While living there, Cheyenne Mountain State Park completed a trail that went to the top of Cheyenne Mountain, so in July of 2020 I hiked it with a close friend. It was one of the hardest hikes I've ever done (mostly because we did an extra loop to see an old homestead that wasn't worth it, which made our hike about 18 miles total, and I ran out of water by roughly mile 8). However, about 3-4 miles into our hike, in the early morning hours, a little yellow, red, and black bird flew right up to me and landed on a branch nearby. I, of course, gasped and froze in my tracks as I watched a handsome western tanager hop around looking for his breakfast while keeping an eye on me. It was definitely one of my favorite birding moments.

4. Painted Bunting

My fourth favorite bird I've seen is the Painted Bunting. Usually, when I show people a picture of a painted bunting, they think it's either not real or that the picture has been way overphotoshopped and the colors aren't possibly that bright. While photos of painted buntings are usually touched up so their colors stand out more, I can say that they really do look that bright and beautiful in person! I was lucky enough to see painted buntings on a somewhat regular basis while living in Georgia. We would hike a lot at a nature reserve called Phinizy Swamp, and my kids and I would go looking for alligators (which we saw a lot of!) and painted buntings. We knew where they usually had their nests, so sometimes we would try and draw them out by playing a painted bunting call on my phone. However, some birders frown on this because overuse can stress the birds, which is why we only did it occasionally. I didn't enjoy living in Georgia very much, but I would love to go back if only to see the painted buntings again!

5. Steller's Jay

The Steller's Jay! This was easily one of my favorite birds while living in Colorado! I only ever saw steller's jays when I was out hiking. My first moment being somewhat near a steller's jay was while I was hiking up Pikes Peak. I had the crazy idea of hiking to the top of the mountain (elevation 14,111 feet!) one year after having heart failure when I had my fourth baby. It was definitely the hardest hike I've ever done, but I made it to the top! Roughly 5 miles into the hike, a steller's jay flew along the trail with me for a while. I like to think he was encouraging me along the way. Another favorite encounter with a steller's jay was when I made it to the top of the Manitou Springs Incline (a 1-mile stairway up the side of a mountain. A total of 2,768 steps and 2,000 feet of elevation gain). Both times when I made it to the top, I sat down for a snack and to feed the friendly chipmunks when a steller's jay flew down by me and squawked till I tossed him half a grape. I'm slowly getting closer to my goal of being Snow White, where all the little woodland creatures greet me while I'm in the woods!

6. Loggerhead Shrike

For my sixth favorite bird I've seen, I picked the Loggerhead Shrike. I didn't even know this bird existed until I looked out into my backyard in Georgia and saw one sitting on my fence. After identifying it, I fell down the rabbit hole of information about these birds. One really cool trait of shrikes is that they store their uneaten prey by impaling it on a thorn or barbed wire so they can return and eat it later. They mostly eat larger insects, rodents, and small birds. The first time I saw one of these guys, he was feasting on a large grasshopper. I noticed him hanging around for a few days, and I spotted a couple places where he impaled other grasshoppers along the barbed wire that ran around the retention pond behind my yard. Loggerhead shrikes are fierce birds, and I loved having one visit my yard and learning all about them, plus they look like little bandits!

7. Scissor-tailed Flycatcher

I didn't realize that my #7 bird, the Scissor-tailed Flycatcher, was so special until I started thinking about it being my favorite bird to see while I lived in Fort Hood, TX, for 7 long years. Scissor-tailed flycatchers have a pretty small range where they breed and winter. Central Texas doesn't have many pretty birds, so these easily topped the list. I always saw one near my home. I'd go on a run, and there was always one that flew along with me and would land ahead of me on a fence till I caught up. I didn't think they were too special besides their cool-looking tails until I lived in Georgia and other birders lost their minds when a scissor-tailed flycatcher was spotted in nearby South Carolina. I feel very lucky that I got to see them on an almost daily basis while I lived in Texas!

8. Bald Eagle

I almost feel a little cliche putting a Bald Eagle on my top 10 list, but there is something magical about watching one fly above you in the wild. After living in Georgia for about a year, a mated pair of bald eagles made a home for themselves in our favorite hiking spot, Phinizy Swamp. One time, my girlfriends and I saw one fly past us with a fish in its beak. Another time, just a few months ago (in Missouri), I was driving when a bald eagle flew out of the trees and flew just over and ahead of me before veering off and into more woods on the other side of the highway. It was quite the experience, and I slowed way down just to have a couple extra seconds watching this beautiful bird soar right ahead of me (and thankfully I didn't have anyone behind me)! Since moving back to Missouri, we've been having more and more bald eagle sightings in my area, which is very exciting to me!

9. Belted Kingfisher

The Belted Kingfisher is a bird I've known about for a while but hadn't been able to find in the wild because they're shy and elusive. The first time I saw one was in Georgia while I was kayaking with a friend, and a kingfisher flew in the trees along the river we were on. Then, after moving to Missouri, we ended up having a belted kingfisher frequent the pond by the house all winter! I haven't had the opportunity to watch one dive into the water to catch a meal, but you can find really cool pictures of them catching a fish online. Even though these birds are shy and fly away as soon as I see them, they're still one of my favorites to see in the wild, plus check out those feathers on their heads! Talk about a cool hairstyle!

10. Broad-tailed Hummingbird

Last but definitely not least is the Broad-tailed Hummingbird. While living in Colorado, I had regular visits to my feeders from four species of hummingbirds, but the most common was the broad-tailed. Originally, I didn't think much of them because they look similar to the ruby-throated hummingbirds that we get here in Missouri, until I noticed a unique sound coming from them while I was hiking. The male broad-tailed hummingbirds make a loud, metallic trill sound with their wingtips when they fly, which I find so interesting. The visitors to my feeders started getting used to me and would come sit on my feeder while I held it. Broad-tailed hummingbirds are also quite resilient, despite their size. They breed mostly in the mountains, go up to 10,000 ft in elevation, and can survive the harsh, cold snow. On September 9, 2020, we got 4-5 inches of snow, and when I looked out in the morning, I saw a broad-tailed hummingbird eating at my feeder! Hummingbirds are some of my favorite species of birds, but the broad-tailed is probably my favorite!
